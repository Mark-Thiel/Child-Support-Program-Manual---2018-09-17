Signing an Acknowledgment of Paternity (AOP)

Workers should check the Putative Father Registry (PFR) to see whether there is a signed and filed AOP or an Order of Filiation for the child. If not (and if the child was born out of wedlock), paternity needs to be established.  

●   See also:  "What is the Putative Father Registry?"

Prior to signing an AOP, the parents must be advised — verbally and in writing — of their rights and of the consequences of signing such an acknowledgment.

●   See also:  Hospital Based Acknowledgment of Paternity — Video (.wmv format, 16 mins) on childsupport.ny.gov.

  1. What is an AOP?

    • An AOP is a legal document signed by the biological father and the unmarried mother of a child who are acknowledging that they are the parents of that child.

    • An AOP has the same force and effect as an Order of Filiation. Once signed, no further judicial or administrative proceedings are required to ratify an unchallenged acknowledgment of paternity.

    • Prior to signing an AOP, the parents must be advised — verbally and in writing — of their rights and of the consequences of signing such an acknowledgment.

      1. Verbal/Oral notice can be provided through an audio or video presentation of the "Acknowledgment of Paternity Form" video located at:  http://www.trainingspace.org/materials/child_support_videos/paternity.wvx.  Also, each hospital has a copy of the Acknowledgment of Paternity video.

      2. The AOP itself satisfies the written notice requirement.

    • The AOP is available in a variety of languages for reference purposes.  However, only the completed English-language can be submitted.

  2. How and where to obtain an AOP

    The AOP is available in hard copy in your district office.  Your supervisor can order more, if needed, from the NYS Office of Temporary and Disability Assistance.  The form can also be found online at:  https://www.childsupport.ny.gov/dcse/pdfs/4418.pdf.

  3. Rights and consequences of signing an AOP

    An AOP that has been voluntarily signed by both parents has the same legal force and effect as a court order determining the child's legal father and establishes the duty of both parents to provided support for the child. This means that if the AOP is not challenged, the parties do not have to go to a court or administrative proceeding to determine or confirm the child's father.

    When a man signs an AOP:

      • He gives up his right to a court hearing to determine if he is the child's father

      • He may establish custody and visitation rights

      • He may be required to give his consent before the child can be placed for adoption

      • It establishes the child's right to inherit from the father if the AOP is filed with the registrar in the district where the birth certificate was filed

      • It may establish the child's right to inherit from the father if the AOP is only filed with the Putative Father Registry

    The child may have the last name of either party. The child's name will not affect the child's legal status.

      • Either parent has the right to consult an attorney before making such an acknowledgment.

      • By executing an acknowledgment on the issue of paternity, the PF waives his right to a hearing to which he would otherwise be entitled.

      • The AOP will be filed with the Putative Father Registry and may establish the child's right to inheritance from the PF/NCP.  (See:  "What is the Putative Father Registry?")

      • If the PF/NCP does not make such an acknowledgment, he can be held liable for support only if the Family Court, after a hearing, makes an order declaring that the PF/NCP is the father of the child and issues an order of support.

      • The written notification to the PF/NCP of his rights and the consequences of signing an AOP must include language similar to that contained in SSL 111-k.

      • If the PF/NCP is not advised of the above rights and consequences, a judge can dismiss the case. [SSL 111-k]  

      • The PF/NCP may establish custody and visitation rights.

      • The PF/NCP may be required to give his consent before the child can be placed for adoption.

    The acknowledgment must be reduced to writing and filed with the Registrar of Births in the district in which the birth occurred and where the birth certificate was filed.

  4. What if the either parent has any doubts about signing the AOP?

    If either parent has any doubts regarding paternity, the AOP should not be signed, and paternity should be established through a court hearing.

  5. Important requirements when completing an AOP

      • Use blue or black ink only to complete the form

      • Do not use correction fluid

      • Do not cross out mistakes in the child section of the form. If the child's name has been crossed out and/or changed in any way, you must start over with a new form.

      • Mistakes in the parents' information (street, apartment number, ZIP code) can be crossed out and corrected, but only if done properly. The mistake must be crossed out with a single line. The correct information must be entered and initialed.

      • Do not leave blank boxes or missing information

    If any of the above errors occur, the form will be rejected.  If you make a mistake while filling out the child section of the AOP, you must start with a brand-new form.

      • The AOP must be completed, signed, and witnessed by two people unrelated to the father or the mother.

      • If the father or mother has no Social Security number, he/she can enter zeroes in the SSN field of the AOP.

      • If the child does not yet have a name when the parents fill out the  AOP, the parents can enter "unnamed" in the field for the child's name.

      • If the father or mother has no fixed address, he/she can enter "homeless" or "undomiciled" in the address field. If this person is residing at a homeless (or other) shelter, he/she may enter the address of the shelter in the address field.

  6. Can an AOP be filled out at the CSEU offices?

    An AOP can be filled out at a CSEU from New York or any other state, as long as the AOP is filed in accordance with the rules of the state here the child was born.

  7. Where does the AOP go once it's signed?

    The AOP form must be filed with the Registrar in the same city, town, or village where the child was born.

    For example:

      1. Emily was born in Albany Medical Center hospital.

      2. Her parents did not sign the AOP in the hospital, but later they come to the CSEU and want to sign the form.

      3. The CSEU must file the AOP with the registrar in the City of Albany.

      4. The City of Albany Registrar will then file the form with the Department of Health, Vital Records Division, and the Putative Father Registry (PFR).

    If the child is born in New York City, the AOP will be filed with the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, Office of Vital Records and the Putative Father Registry (PFR).

    (See "What is the Putative Father Registry?" at:  https://ocfs.ny.gov/main/publications/Pub5040.pdf)

  8. Can the parents rescind an AOP after they have already signed it?

    Either parent can petition the court to vacate the AOP within the earlier of 60 days of signing the form or the date of an administrative or judicial proceeding related to the child.

    For the purposes of determining the deadline for rescinding an AOP, the date of an administrative or judicial proceeding is the date by which the PF/NCP is required to answer the petition.

    If the parent is under 18 years of age when signing the AOP, the parent must challenge the AOP within 60 days after that parent's 18th birthday.

    The following are possible legitimate reasons why a petition to rescind and AOP might be approved:

        • The AOP was signed under duress.

        • The AOP was signed fraudulently, or was fraudulently produced.

        • There is a material mistake of fact on the form.

  9. When a court vacates an acknowledgment of Paternity

    When a court vacates an AOP, it sends a copy of the order vacating the AOP to the NYS Department of Health (DOH) and the PFR -- or, in New York City, to the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene and the PFR.

    The DOH or the Dept of Health and Mental Hygiene sends the mother a new birth certificate without the name of the father on it.
