Introduction to Paternity Establishment

Paternity establishment activities must be initiated as soon as possible

 based upon available information.

When a custodial parent is referred to or applies for child support services, workers must determine if paternity has been established. Paternity establishment is the process through which the father of a child born out of wedlock is identified and legally determined.

Paternity establishment is vital to all other functions of the child support program; we can’t go any further without it. If paternity is not established, we aren’t able to pursue a child support order, and therefore, we can’t collect child support payments.

In the Child Support Program, our focus is on legal fatherhood. By understanding the concept of what a “legal father” is and how legal fatherhood is determined, you will be able to confidently assist parents in establishing paternity for their child. You will also be able to help educate both custodial and noncustodial parents on their rights and responsibilities.

  1. Definitions of fatherhood

      1. "Legal father" refers to a man recognized by law as the male parent of a child. A man is considered the legal father of a child if he was married to the child's biological mother at the time of the child's birth, or if paternity has been established through legal adoption, an Order of Filiation, or by signing an AOP.

      2. "Biological father" is the man who provided the paternal genes to the child.  A biological father is sometimes called a "birth parent" or "birth father."

      3. A "putative father" is a man whose legal relationship to a child has not been established but who is alleged to be or claims that he may be the biological father of a child.

  2. There are three ways in which legal paternity of a child can be established

      1. If the mother is married at any time during the conception, pregnancy, or the time of the birth of the child, her husband is the legal father of the child.

      2. If both parents sign an AOP, this has the same force and effect as an Order of Filiation (i.e. it legally establishes paternity).

      1. If paternity is adjudicated and established in court, the court will issue an Order of Filiation. (This may be preceded by a genetic marker test.)

    * Approximately 43% of children in NYS are born out of wedlock. Of those, in 68% of cases, the parents sign an AOP form at the hospital. (2018 figures)

  3. How does the worker know whether paternity needs to be established?

    Source:  Fed Cert Doc

      1. Workers can check the Putative Father Registry (PFR) to find out whether an AOP or an Order of Filiation has been filed or issued for an child. If not (and if the child was born out of wedlock), paternity needs to be established for that child.

    (See:  "What is the Putative Father Registry?")

      1. Workers can ask the customer for a copy of the child's birth certificate.

    Once the case is built:

      1. Workers can check the Worker Alerts > Management Mode > Overage Statuses tab daily to see any cases needing paternity establishment.  Cases needing paternity establishment will report a P Group as an open status.)

      1. A Code 3, Code 4, Code 7, Code 9 in the Wedlock Indicator field on the Case Mode > Child tab indicates the need to establish paternity (and a P Group will automatically be set on the Case Mode > Status tab).

  4. Deadlines for Establishing Paternity

      • Paternity establishment proceedings can be initiated at any time before the child reaches 21 years of age. [FCA 517]

      • Paternity establishment proceedings can be initiated after the child reaches 21 years of age if the PF/NCP has previously acknowledged paternity in writing or furnished support. [FCA 517]

  1. Impact on Performance Measures

    Establishing paternity is one of the first steps to the success of all the functions that follow. The more cases where paternity is established, the better your district's performance measures will be.

    Paternity establishment has direct impact on the following performance measures:

        • PEP — Calculated by the number of paternities established divided by the number of children born out-of-wedlock.

        • SEP — The court cannot issue an Order of Support unless paternity has been established
