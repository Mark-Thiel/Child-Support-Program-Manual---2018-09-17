Interstate laws

The primary law governing interstate case processing is UIFSA (Uniform Interstate Family Support Act). Every state was required to adopt UIFSA into its state law, by 1996 federal regulations, so as to make interstate case processing clearly standardized throughout the U.S. Subsequent regulations have required each state to adopt an updated version of the UIFSA, referred to as UIFSA 2008, into its state law. New York's UIFSA statute is Article 5-B of the Family Court Act.

UIFSA includes provisions specifying that it is to be read in combination with other state laws. For example, UIFSA sets forth the process for establishing an order in an interstate case, but does not dictate the calculation for setting a support obligation; therefore, that calculation would be governed by the law of the state where the order is established.

In addition to UIFSA and other state law, there is a 1994 federal law called the Full Faith and Credit for Child Support Orders Act, which simply duplicated some but not all sections of UIFSA. The 1996 federal regulation requiring all states to adopt UIFSA made the Full Faith and Credit Act essentially redundant, though it continues to be in effect.
