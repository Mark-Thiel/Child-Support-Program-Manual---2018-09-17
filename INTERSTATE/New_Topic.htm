Other Points to be Aware Of

Beyond the information contained in Interstate Case Assistant and in other sections of this chapter, there are factors that affect cases themselves and your processing of them:

  • Nondisclosure affidavit: In addition to the safeguards that apply to all cases, this is an extra measure of safety that UIFSA provides for parties and children in interstate cases. In New York law, it is covered in FCA 580-312. Information about when and how to use it is incorporated in Interstate Case Assistant at the points where it is appropriate.

A tribunal that receives a nondisclosure affidavit must prevent identifying information about the party or child from being disclosed to the other party or the public. The other party has the right to request a hearing in that tribunal to attempt to have the information disclosed; the tribunal must then base its decision on the interests of justice.

  • Establishing orders: Calculation of the support obligation and duration of support are set by the laws of the state issuing the order.

  • Modifying orders: Only one state at a time has the authority to modify the order. (That state can be identified by using Interstate Case Assistant.) This authority is called continuing, exclusive jurisdiction (CEJ).

  • Enforcing orders: Interest on arrears is calculated according to the law of the state that issued the order.

  • Case closure: Several of the state and federal criteria for case closure are specific to interstate cases. These criteria are incorporated in the automated case closure process, so if you keep the case record up to date as a standard part of case processing, an interstate case will be system-selected for case closure when appropriate in the same way as a local case would be.

  • Problems with other states: If you encounter a problem with another state that you can't resolve, you'll need to provide precise an thorough detail about it. This includes specifying to the extent possible the state; the entity (child support agency, court, etc.); the office (name of county, parish, borough, etc.); the individual; the date and time (or sequence of dates); and what exactly they did that they shouldn't have, or didn't do that they should have. Using that detail, discuss the issue with your supervisor or coordinator, who can then contact your DCSS county representative if necessary.
