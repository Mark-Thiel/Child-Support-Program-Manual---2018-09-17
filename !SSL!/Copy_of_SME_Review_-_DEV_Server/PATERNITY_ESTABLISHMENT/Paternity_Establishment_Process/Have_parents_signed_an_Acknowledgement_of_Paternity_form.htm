Signing an Acknowledgement of Paternity (AOP)

Prior to signing an AOP, the Putative Father must be advised — verbally and in writing — of his rights and of the consequences of signing such an acknowledgement.

Source: 1980s Manual

See also:  Video (in .wmv format, 16 minutes long) on childsupport.ny.gov:  Hospital Based Acknowledgment of Paternity

Workers should check the PFR to see whether there is a signed AOP or an Order of Filiation for the child. If not (and if the child was born out of wedlock), paternity needs to be established.

  1. What is an AOP?

    • An AOP is a legal document signed by the biological father and the unmarried mother of a child who are acknowledging that they are the parents of that child.

    • An AOP has the same force and effect as an Order of Filiation. Once signed, no further judicial or administrative proceedings are required to ratify an unchallenged acknowledgement of paternity.

    • Prior to signing an AOP, the PF/NCP must be advised — verbally and in writing — of his rights and of the consequences of signing such an acknowledgement.

      1. Verbal/Oral notice can be provided through an audio or video presentation of the "Acknowledgement of Paternity Form" video located at:  http://www.trainingspace.org/materials/child_support_videos/paternity.wvx.  Also, each hospital has a copy of the Acknowledgement of Paternity video.

      2. The AOP itself satisfies the written notice requirement.

    • The AOP is available in a variety of languages.  However, only the completed English-language form is admissible.

    • Approximately 42% of children in NYS are born out of wedlock. Of those, in 67% of cases, the parents sign an AOP form at the hospital. (2018 figures)

  2. How and where to obtain an AOP

    The AOP is available in hard copy in your district office.  Your supervisor can order more, if needed, from the NYS Office of Temporary and Disability Assistance.  The form can also be found online at:  https://www.childsupport.ny.gov/dcse/pdfs/4418.pdf.

  3. Rights and consequences of signing an AOP

    The rights of the PF/NCP and consequences of signing an AOP (as listed in SSL 111-k) are as follows:

      • The PF has the right to consult an attorney before making such an acknowledgement.

      • By executing an acknowledgement on the issue of paternity, the PF waives his right to a hearing to which he would otherwise be entitled.

      • The PF's AOP will be filed with the Putative Father Registry and may establish the child's right to inheritance from the PF/NCP.

      • If the PF/NCP does not make such an acknowledgement, he can be held liable for support only if the Family Court, after a hearing, makes an order declaring that the PF/NCP is the father of the child and issues an order of support.

      • The written notification to the PF/NCP of his rights and the consequences of signing an AOP must include language similar to that contained in SSL 111-k.

      • If the PF/NCP is not advised of the above rights and consequences, a judge can dismiss the case. [SSL 111-k]  

    The acknowledgement must be reduced to writing and filed with the Registrar of Births in the district in which the birth occurred and where the birth certificate was filed.

  4. What if the PF/NCP has any doubts about signing the AOP?

    If either parent has any doubts regarding paternity, the AOP should not be signed, and paternity should be established through a court hearing.

  5. Important requirements when completing an AOP

      • Use blue or black ink only to complete the form

      • Do not use correction fluid

      • Do not cross out mistakes in the child section of the form.

      • Do not leave blank boxes or missing information

    If any of the above errors occur, the form will be rejected.  If you make a mistake while filling out the child section of the AOP, you must start with a brand-new form.

     

      • The AOP must be completed, signed, and witnessed by two people unrelated to the father or the mother.

      • If the father or mother has no Social Security number, he/she can enter zeroes in the SSN field of the AOP.

      • If the child does not yet have a name when the parents fill out the  AOP, the parents can enter "unnamed" in the field for the child's name.

      • If the father or mother has no fixed address, he/she can enter "homeless" or "undomiciled" in the address field. If this person is residing at a homeless (or other) shelter, he/she may enter the address of the shelter in the address field.

     

  6. Can an AOP be filled out at the CSEU offices?

    An AOP can be filled out at a CSEU only if the child was born in New York State. Districts should follow local protocols, since procedures vary among districts.

    If the form is signed at the offices of the CSEU, the CSEU must keep a copy of the AOP in the case record, and send the original to the Birth Registrar's office.

  7. Where does the AOP go once it's signed?

    The AOP form must be filed with the Registrar in the same city, town, or village where the birth certificate was issued (i.e. in the same city, town, or village as the hospital where the child was born).

    For example:

      1. Emily was born in Albany Medical Center hospital.

      2. Her parents did not sign the AOP in the hospital, but later they come to the CSEU and want to sign the form.

      3. The CSEU must file the AOP with the registrar in the City of Albany.

      4. The City of Albany Registrar will then file the form with the Department of Health, Vital Records Division, and the Putative Father Registry (PFR).

  8. Can the parents rescind an AOP after they have already signed it?

    Either parent can petition the court to vacate the AOP within the earlier of 60 days of signing the form or the date of an administrative or judicial proceeding related to the child.

    For the purposes of determining the deadline for rescinding an AOP, the date of an administrative or judicial proceeding is the date by which the PF/NCP is required to answer the petition.

    If the parent is under 18 years of age when signing the AOP, the parent must challenge the AOP within 60 days after that parent's 18th birthday.

    The following are possible legitimate reasons why a petition to rescind and AOP might be approved:

        • The AOP was signed under duress.

        • The AOP was signed fraudulently, or was fraudulently produced.

        • There is a material mistake of fact on the form.

  9. When a court vacates an Acknowledgement of Paternity

    When a court vacates an AOP, it sends a copy of the order vacating the AOP to the Department of Health (DOH).

    The DOH sends the information to the office of the Registrar, which officially seals the AOP and mails the customer a new birth certificate without the name of the person who had mistakenly, fraudulently, or unwillingly signed the AOP.
