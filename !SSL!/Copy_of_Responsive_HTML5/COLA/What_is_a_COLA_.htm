Step 1:    What is a COLA?

 

A COLA is a cost-of-living adjustment. It is a way to provide for periodic increases in the amount of child support that a CP receives in order to keep up with increases in the cost of living in the United States (as measured by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics).

 

The CSEU has the authority to issue COLAs administratively (i.e. without the need to go through the courts), provided that:

 

  • all eligibility criteria are met

 

AND

 

  • neither the CP nor the NCP (nor the SCU) objects to the increase.

 

The ASSETS system itself automatically recognizes when a case is eligible for a COLA, and then:

 

If the CP is on TA ASSETS automatically notifies the CP and NCP that a COLA will automatically be applied to their case unless one of them objects. Either party has the right to object to a COLA. If one of them does, a court hearing is held to determine whether the COLA goes through.
If the CP is NOT on TA ASSETS automatically notifies the CP and NCP that they have the right to request a COLA. Either party has the right to request it. If neither party requests a COLA, no action is taken. If a request is made, either party (even the one who made the request) has the right to object to the COLA. If one of them does object, a court hearing is held to determine whether the COLA goes through.
If the NCP is on TA

The case is not eligible for a COLA. Note that ASSETS will only know whether or not the NCP is on TA if his/her CAN and CIN numbers are both in ASSETS. If they aren’t (for example, if the NCP is on TA in a different state), ASSETS will not know that the NCP is on TA unless the Delinquency Switch is a 19 – which the caseworker would have had to set manually.

If ASSETS doesn’t know that the NCP is on TA, ASSETS will send COLA eligibility notices to both parties, and it would be up to the NCP to object to the COLA based upon the fact that s/he is on TA.
