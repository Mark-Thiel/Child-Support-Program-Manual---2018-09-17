Introduction — The Final Rule

Financial Investigation Requirements under the Amended State and Federal Regulations March 27, 2018:

State regulations at 18 NYCRR 347.8 and 347.9 were amended to reflect program requirements resulting from the adoption of the federal Flexibility, Efficiency, and Modernization in Child Support Enforcement Programs (Final Rule), dated December 20, 2016. The State regulations were amended to guidelines for financial investigations in establishment, modification, and enforcement of child support obligations. Information about the revisions may be found in 17-ADM-11.

  1. FINANCIAL INVESTIGATIONS:  LAWS AND REGULATIONS

    1. Recording Case Information

      1. Federal Regulation:

      45 CFR §303.4(b)(4). The final rule requires that child support agencies maintain documentation in the case record of information and evidence regarding the noncustodial parent’s (NCP’s) specific circumstances. This includes information acquired during the financial investigation and considered in determining the amount of imputed income.

      2.  State Regulations:

      347.8(a)(1)(iii) Maintain in the automated case record any information from new or modified orders of support, including the factual basis for the support obligation.

      347.8(a)(2)(v). Create a record of the information or documents obtained as a result of the financial investigation.

      347.8(a)(4)  In any proceeding to establish, modify, or enforce an order of child support, the child support enforcement unit shall review the record for information obtained from the financial investigation conducted on the noncustodial parent which may assist the court in making a factual determination regarding the noncustodial parent’s ability to pay child support in accordance with the standards set forth in section 240 of the DRL and section 413 of the FCA and provide such information to the court.

      3.  Impact

      The amendments expand the information about new or modified orders of support to be maintained in the automated case record to include information about the factual basis for the support obligation. The amendments also provide for the creation of a record of the information or documents obtained as a result of the financial investigation conducted by the support collection unit or child support enforcement unit (collectively referred to hereafter as the “CSEU”).

      The CSEU may use a checklist of sources of income, assets, and specific circumstances of the NCP to document and record information searched and/or obtained as a part of the financial investigation. The checklist should be retained in the case records (e.g., the ASSETS Document Log). A sample checklist/guidance document, the Financial Disclosure Desk Aid, is available on the Location page of the Electronic Resource System (ERS). See 17-ADM-11.

      Information about the factual basis for the support obligation, must be documented in the remarks of the case records and/or on a document electronically saved on ASSETS (e.g., Findings of Fact). In addition, the CSEU must update the Child Support Standards Act (CSSA) Information record in ASSETS to include information about the new or modified order.

      The CSEU is also required to provide the court with any information from the case record which may assist the court in making a factual determination regarding the NCP’s ability to pay or comply with the order of support. Obtaining and presenting this information in admissible form may require the assistance of counsel.

    1. Financial Investigations

      1. Federal Regulation

      45 CFR § 303.4(b) Child support agencies are required to review all available sources of information and document the sources that were reviewed, even if unsuccessful in discovering evidence of a parent’s income or ability to pay. The obligation should be based on evidence of the NCP’s ability to pay to the extent there is sufficient information known or available. In the preamble of the final rule, OCSE expressed a concern that orders for low-income parents are not based on the facts of the case but may be based upon a standardized amount above the means of the parent to pay.

      45 CFR § 303.6(c)(4). Child support agencies must establish guidelines for the use of civil contempt in IV-D cases. These guidelines must require that the IV-D agency screen the case record for information regarding the NCP’s ability to pay or comply with the order. The child support agency must also provide the court with information regarding the NCP’s ability to pay, or comply with the order, and his or her ability to pay the purge amount or comply with the purge conditions.

      2.  State Regulations

      347.8(a)(2) Prior to any proceeding to establish, modify, or enforce an order of child support, the child support enforcement unit shall take reasonable steps to determine the noncustodial parent’s income, financial circumstances, and ability to pay support in accordance with the child support standards set forth in section 240 of the DRL and section 413 of the FCA, by conducting a financial investigation pursuant to the following guidelines:

            1. Review available local, State, and federal information sources pursuant to section 347.7(b) of this Part, including, as appropriate, electronic data from other governmental agencies, State and national directories of new hires, and State and other tax information.

            2. Obtain information by case conferencing, interviews with either or both parties, mandatory financial disclosure, or questionnaires, as appropriate.

            3. Obtain information from the noncustodial parent or third parties using subpoenas or requests pursuant to sections 111-p, 111-r, and 111-s of the Social Services Law or article 31 of the Civil Practice Law and Rules, as appropriate.

      347.8(a)(4) In any proceeding to establish, modify, or enforce an order of child support, the child support enforcement unit shall review the record for information obtained from the financial investigation conducted on the noncustodial parent which may assist the court in making a factual determination regarding the noncustodial parent’s ability to pay child support in accordance with the standards set forth in section 240 of the DRL and section 413 of the FCA and provide such information to the court.

      347.8(c)(3)(ii). The child support enforcement unit must review the case file and provide the court with information contained therein regarding the noncustodial parent’s ability to pay, or otherwise comply with the child support order, which may assist the court in making a factual determination regarding the noncustodial parent’s ability to pay a purge amount or comply with any conditions set in a hearing pursuant to sections 454 or 455 of the FCA.

      3.  Impact

      The CSEU must take reasonable steps to determine the NCP’s income, financial circumstances, and ability to pay support before commencing a proceeding to establish, modify, or enforce a support obligation. This does not prevent the CSEU from investigation or discovery activities after the proceeding has commenced, especially if the circumstances or facts (or lack of facts) of the case merit further action.

      The regulations require that the CSEU: (i) screen the case for information regarding the NCP’s ability to pay or otherwise comply with the order; (ii) utilize other investigation or discovery methods if appropriate; and iii) provide the court with information regarding the NCP’s ability to pay or comply with the order. Providing the information in admissible form may require the assistance of counsel.

      Determining what level of financial investigation is appropriate and reasonable depends on the facts of the case and the parties’ circumstances. For example, if the NCP has stable employment, lives within his or her income, and the CP does not indicate any other sources of income. the mandatory financial disclosure required by FCA 424-a may be sufficient. If the NCP is self-employed, has expenses exceeding reported income, or the CP indicates that the NCP has unreported income or assets, a more searching inquiry may be needed.

      In enforcement actions, the CSEU must review the case file and provide the court with any information from the case record which may assist the court in making a factual determination regarding the NCP’s: 1) ability to pay the support obligation or otherwise comply with the order of support; or 2) ability to pay a purge amount or comply with any conditions set in a hearing pursuant to sections 454 or 455 of the FCA.

    2. Calculation of Support Obligation in Absence of Information About Ability to Earn

      1. Federal Regulation:

      45 CFR § 302.56. The federal regulation does not prohibit states from imputing income. However, if imputation of income is permitted, the guidelines must take into consideration the specific circumstances of the noncustodial to the extent known, including factors like the ones listed in the amended state regulation.

      2. State Regulation

      347.8(a)(2)(iv.)  If there is insufficient evidence of the noncustodial parent’s income or financial circumstances to use as the measure of the noncustodial parent’s ability to pay, then any recommendation by the child support enforcement unit regarding the support obligation shall be based on available information about the specific circumstances of the noncustodial parent, including assets, residence, employment and earnings history, job skills, education, literacy, age, health, criminal record or other employment barriers, record of seeking work, job market conditions, the availability of employers willing to hire the noncustodial parent, prevailing earnings level, and other relevant background  factors in the case to the extent such information is available and relevant.

      3.  Impact

      If there is insufficient direct evidence of the NCP’s income or financial circumstances to use as the measure of the NCP’s ability to pay, the court may be asked to impute income. NYS law current requires that any order based on imputed income be supported by information in the court record.

      The federal Final Rule requires that any recommendation by the CSEU regarding imputation of a support obligation be based on available information about the specific circumstances of the NCP, to the extent such information is available and relevant. The amended regulation lists the types of information that may be helpful to the court. imputation of a support obligation be based on available information about the specific circumstances of the NCP, to the extent such information is available and relevant. The amended regulation lists the types of information that may be helpful to the court.

    3. Responsibility to Modify if Financial Investigations Indicates a Change in Circumstances

      1. State Regulation:

      347.8(c)(2)(ii) If a review of the terms of the order of child support and/or the results of the financial investigation demonstrate a change in the parties’ financial or factual circumstances such that modification of the support obligation may be appropriate, the child support enforcement unit must immediately petition or otherwise assist the recipient of services to petition the Family Court or the Supreme Court, if the Supreme Court has retained jurisdiction over support issues in the case, for modification of the order of support.

      2. Impact and explanation of other changes

      The amendment to 347.8(c)(2)(ii) requires the CSEU to petition or otherwise assist the recipient of services to modify the order of support if a change in the parties’ financial or factual circumstances indicate that modification of the support obligation may be appropriate A petition may be filed for either an upward or downward modification.

    4. Enforcement -Notice

      1. Federal Regulation:

      45 CFR § 303.6(c)(4)(iii). Child support agencies are required to provide clear notice to the NCP that the ability to pay constitutes the critical question in a contempt or violation proceeding.

      2. State Regulation:

      347.8(c)(3)(i) Where an order of support payable to the custodial parent is not being complied with, the child support enforcement unit must ensure that any petition to the Family Court or the Supreme Court, if the Supreme Court has retained jurisdiction over support issues in the case, alleges a violation of the order of support, includes a request for the adjudication of the delinquency amount and the award of a money judgment, and provides clear notice to the noncustodial parent that his or her inability to pay child support as ordered is a defense to a finding of willful violation or contempt.

      347.9(d) Additional enforcement action.

      ... Every petition to find the noncustodial parent in violation or motion to hold a noncustodial parent in contempt of an order to pay child support shall provide clear notice to the noncustodial parent that his or her inability to pay child support as ordered by the court is a defense to a finding of willful violation or her inability to pay child support as ordered by the court is a defense to a finding of willful violation or contempt.…

      3. Impact:

      Every petition to find the NCP in violation, or motion in supreme court to hold a NCP in contempt, of an order to pay child support provide clear notice that the ability to pay constitutes the critical question in violation or contempt proceedings against the NCP. The petitions available through the Document Generation module of ASSETS include enhanced warning language providing clear notice to the NCP that his or her ability to pay constitutes the critical question.

  1. GETTING INFORMATION FROM THE FILE TO THE COURTROOM

     

    Most of the information found during the financial investigation will be hearsay.  Hearsay is: 1) a statement (oral or written); 2) made out of court; 3) that is being offered for the truth of the facts asserted in the statement. Hearsay is inadmissible unless it falls within an exception to the rule.

    1. Admissions and Declarations

        1. Admissions against interest

      Any statement by a party may be used to prove a fact in the case against him or her. Party admissions are outside the hearsay rule. Rothman v. McLaughlin & Stern, 127 A.D. 3d 591.

      a. Admission in favor of the party offering them are not admissible. People v. Ramlall, 99 A.D. 3d 815.

      b. Under some circumstance, a party may adopt a statement by silence. People v. Rogers, 94 A.D. 3d 1246.

        1. Declarations Against Interest

      A statement by a non-party is admissible if:

        1. The declarant is unavailable;

        2. The declaration is against the pecuniary, proprietary or penal interest of the declarant;

        3. The declarant had competent knowledge of the facts

        4. The declarant had no probable motive to misrepresent the facts.

        5. Town of Bethel v. Howard, 95 A.D. 3d 1489 (Internet posting as declarations against interest)

        1. Judicial admissions

      Statements made or legal positions taken in prior proceedings are admissible against the party that made them. Kosturek v. Kosturek, 107 A.D. 3d 762; Goldman v. Malagie, 45 Misc. 3d 37.

       

    1. Spontaneous Declaration or Excited Utterance and Present Sense Impression

        1. Spontaneous declaration or excited utterance

            1. A declaration following an exciting or traumatic event

            2. Made while the declarant is still under stress of excitement or shock

            3. The stress or shock must be sufficient to still the declarant’s ability to reflect or prevaricate. Kutza v. Bovis Lend Lease, 95 A.D. 3d 590; People v Swinger, 689 N.Y.S. 2d 336.

        2. Present Sense Impression

      See Lee v. New York Hospital Queens, 118 A.D. 3d 750.

       

    1. Testimony at Prior Trial — CPLR 451

      1. Trial of the same subject matter in same or another action between the same parties or their representatives.

      2. Witness must be unavailable as set out in statute

       

    2. Demonstrative Evidence

        1. Photographs,  Video, and Audio

          1. Photos, audio or video recordings must be authenticated by a person familiar with the object/scene.

          2. The witness must testify that the exhibit is a true, correct and/or accurate representation of the object/scene portrayed. However, with videos, in some cases testimony about the making of the video or chain of custody may be required.

          3. Audio recordings must not be altered or edited and the witness must be able to identify the speaker’s voice. Transcripts may be admitted, but if the recording is available it is the best evidence.

          4. Grucci v. Grucci, 20 N.Y. 3d 893; Das v. Sun Wah Restaurant, 94 A.D. 3d 752; People v. France, 120 A.D. 3d 1357; Morreale v. Froelich, 125 A.D. 3d 1280.

        1. Diagrams, Maps, and Models

          1. Must be a true representation of what it purports to represent, if not to scale, it must be accurate and correct for it proposed use (i.e., to illustrate the witness’ testimony, to aid the fact-finder or as direct proof of a fact)

          2. Maps may be admissible as public records (see paragraph F, below).

        2. Experiments and Test Results

      The technique or methodology of the test must be:

          1. Reliable

          2. Generally accepted by the scientific community; and 3) performed under conditions similar to those at the time of the occurrence.

        1. DNA or Genetic Marker Tests — CPLR 4518(d), (e)

          1. The test results, if certified, are prima facie evidence of the facts contained therein.

          2. If the certified test results show a probability of paternity of 95% or greater, the report creates a rebuttable presumption of paternity. If no objections are filed, the report established the paternity of the child.

       

    1. Private Writings

      1. Written agreements between the parties (contracts, leases, options, etc.) are admissible if properly authenticated.

      2. Private Memoranda, letters, journals, etc., if authenticated, are admissible if they meet the requirements as an admission or declaration (see paragraph A, above).

      3. Past recollection recorded – a writing of a witness may be admissible if:

          1.  It was made at or about the time of the act, event or occurrence recorded

          1. The witness can attest NOW that it was accurate at the time it was made

          2. The witness is presently unable to recall the facts.

       

    2. Public Documents, Records, and Reports

      1. Public records include deeds, state agency records, UCC filings, etc.

      2. Public records may be admitted under the Business Records exception, the statutory Public Records exception (CPLR 2307) or the common law exception.

      3. The CPLR requires that public records be “authenticated” (certified) as a true and complete copy of the records or reports contained. A certified public record is “self-authenticating,” that is, it needs no foundation testimony for admittance.

      4. The common law rule applies where a public officer is under a duty by statute or by the nature of his or her office to maintain a record or create a report of the acts or transactions occurring in the course of his or her official duty. The records or reports made under the supervision of his or her office are admissible into evidence. People v. Smith, 258 A.D. 2d 245; People v. Baker, 705 N.Y. 2d 846.

      5. The exception applies to facts, events or data, not opinions.

      6. Foreign public records may be authenticated pursuant to CPLR 4542. Matter of Eggers, 471 NYS2d 570.

       

    3. Computer Records

      1. Under the common law, computer records generally qualified as business records. The person entering the data did not need to be produced to give foundational testimony. People v. Markowitz, 721 N.Y. 2d 758.

      1. In 2002, CPLR 4518(a) was amended to provide rules for the admissibility of computer records. Printouts of computer records are subject to the same 4-part test as any other business record, plus an additional requirement that the printout be certified as a true and accurate representation of the electronic record.

      1. In addition, the court may consider the method or manner by which the electronic record was stored, maintained or retrieved in determining whether the exhibit is a true and accurate representation of such electronic record.

       

    4. Cell Phone Messages and Texts

      1. Cell phone instant messages may be authenticated by a participant in the conversation testifying that the other party’s name came up on his/her phone, and that the “cut and paste” document or photos of the messages offered to the court fairly and accurately represented the exchange between the parties. People v. Agudelo, 96 A.D. 3d 611.

      2. Text messages may be self-authenticating if the content reliably indicates that the other party was the sender. Photos of the texts are admissible if the witness testifies that he/she saw the photos taken and that they accurately and fairly reproduce the texts from the phone screen. People v. Green, 107 A.D. 3d 915.

       

    5. Business Records

        1. General Rule: General Rule: CPLR 4518 (a)

            1. Foundation:

                1. The record was made and kept in the regular course of business

                2. It was the “regular course of business” (an established business practice or procedure) to make the record

                3. The record was made at or near the time of the act or event recorded

                4. The person making the record had a business duty to report or record the data

                5. People v. Ramos, 13 N.Y. 3d 914; People v. Cordova, 127 A.D. 3d 757; Tougher Industries Ins. v. Dormitory Authority, 130 A.D. 3d 1397 (e-mail as a business record).

            2. All other circumstances of the making of the memorandum or record, including lack of personal knowledge by the maker, may be proved to affect its weight, but they shall not affect its admissibility.

            3. However, even if a business record meeting all the requirements of CPLR 4518 (a), it may still be inadmissible if it violates another rule of evidence.

            4. Double Hearsay – If the record contains voluntary hearsay statements made by 3rd persons not engaged in business or under any duty in relation to the business, the exception generally does not apply. People v. Boatwright, 748 N.Y. 2d 541; People v. Reynoso, 73 N.Y. 2d 816

            5. Double hearsay may be admissible in certain circumstances:

              1. If the 3rd party statement is admissible under another rule, the double hearsay is admissible.

              2. Medical records:

                      • Records, reports, including opinions, of other physicians, test results and laboratory reports are admissible if related to the diagnosis or treatment of the patient. Rodriguez v. Piccone, 5 A.D. 3d 757, Moran v. Demarinis, 152 A.D. 2d 546.

                      • Patient statements, including statements about the cause of the accident or injury, are only admissible to the extent they are related to diagnosis, prognosis or treatment. People v. Ortega, 15 N.Y. 3d 610; Matter of Dolan, 35 Misc. 3d 781.

                  1.  If the 3rd party statement is sufficiently “trustworthy,” it may be admissible as part of the record. People v. Settles, 46 N.Y. 2d 154

                1. The substantial familiarity rule:

                  1. The out-of-court statement must have indicia of reliability characteristic of a business record – the elements set out in CPLR 4518.

                  2. The witness must be substantially familiar with the business practices of the entity making the record. The witness does not need to know the specific record keeping practices if he/she is familiar with the statutory or regulatory requirements governing the making of the record or if it is made to suit the witness’ employer’s requirements.

                  3. The witness or the entity holding the record or document must routinely rely on the records of the entity making the records.

                  4. People v. Cratsley, 86 N.Y. 2d 81; People v. DiSalvo, 727 N.Y.S. 2d 146; Portfolio Recovery Assoc. v. Lall, 127 A.D. 3d 576; Deutsche Bank v. Monica, 131 A.D. 3d 737.

           

        1. Hospital Bills – CPLR 4518 (b)

          A hospital bill is admissible in evidence under this rule and is prima facie evidence of the facts contained, provided:

              1. It bears a certification by the head of the hospital or by a responsible employee in the controller’s or accounting office that the bill is correct

              2. Each of the items was necessarily supplied

              3. The amount charged is reasonable

        1. Other Records – CPLR 4518 (c)

          1. All records, writings and other things referred to in sections 2306 and 2307 are admissible in evidence under this rule and are prima facie evidence of the facts contained, provided they bear a certification or authentication by the head of the hospital, laboratory, department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or by an employee delegated for that purpose or by a qualified physician.

          2. CPLR 2306 -records relating to the condition or treatment of a patient held by a hospital, a department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or an officer thereof, requiring the production

          3. CPLR 2307 – records of a library, or a department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or an officer thereof

           

        2. SCU payment and disbursement records – CPLR 4518 (f)

          1. The records are admissible if:

            1. they bear a certification by an official of a social services district attesting to the accuracy of the content of the record or report of support payments;

            2. in attesting to the accuracy of the record or report such official has received confirmation from the department of social services or the fiscal agent that the record or report of support payments reflects the processing of all support payments in the possession of the department or the fiscal agent as of a specified date; and

            3.  the document is a record or report of support payments maintained pursuant to title six-A of article three of the social services law.

          2. If so certified, such record or report shall be admitted into evidence under this rule without the need for additional foundation testimony.

          3. Confirmation of payments requests are made to the processing center, by CMU inquiry via ECS, to confirm that payments for a specific NCP have been processed, and there are no “unprocessed” payments as of a certain date holding with the payment Processing Center or in the fiscal agent account.

          4. Such records shall be the basis for a permissive inference of the facts contained therein unless the trier of fact finds good cause not to draw such inference.

           

        3. Confinement costs – CPLR 4518 (g)

          Confinement costs may be proven in 3 ways:

              1. Medical bills under 4518 (b)

              2. If the bills were paid by Medicaid, as a public record under CPLR 4518 (c) and 2307

              3. Under CPLR 4518 (g) -Any hospital bills or records relating to the costs of pregnancy or birth of a child are admissible in evidence and are prima facie evidence of the facts contained therein, provided they bear a certification or authentication by the head of the hospital, laboratory, department or bureau of a municipal corporation or the state or by an employee designated for that purpose, or by a qualified physician.
