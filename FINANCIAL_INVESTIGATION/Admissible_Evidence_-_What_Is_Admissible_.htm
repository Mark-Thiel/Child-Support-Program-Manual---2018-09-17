Admissible Evidence:  What Is Admissible to Court?

Most of the information found during the financial investigation will be hearsay.  Hearsay is inadmissible unless it falls within an exception to the rule.

Hearsay is: 1) a statement (oral or written); 2) made out of court; 3) that is being offered for the truth of the facts asserted in the statement.

Below are types of admissible financial evidence and the conditions or requirements (e.g. redaction, notarization, authentication) that govern the admissibility of each.

    1. Admissions and Declarations

        1. Admissions against interest

      Any statement by a party may be used to prove a fact in the case against him or her. Party admissions are outside the hearsay rule. Rothman v. McLaughlin & Stern, 127 A.D. 3d 591.

      a. Admission in favor of the party offering them are not admissible. People v. Ramlall, 99 A.D. 3d 815.

      b. Under some circumstance, a party may adopt a statement by silence. People v. Rogers, 94 A.D. 3d 1246.

        1. Declarations Against Interest

      A statement by a non-party is admissible if:

        1. The declarant is unavailable;

        2. The declaration is against the pecuniary, proprietary or penal interest of the declarant;

        3. The declarant had competent knowledge of the facts

        4. The declarant had no probable motive to misrepresent the facts.

        5. Town of Bethel v. Howard, 95 A.D. 3d 1489 (Internet posting as declarations against interest)

        1. Judicial admissions

      Statements made or legal positions taken in prior proceedings are admissible against the party that made them. Kosturek v. Kosturek, 107 A.D. 3d 762; Goldman v. Malagie, 45 Misc. 3d 37.

       

    1. Spontaneous Declaration or Excited Utterance and Present Sense Impression

        1. Spontaneous declaration or excited utterance

            1. A declaration following an exciting or traumatic event

            2. Made while the declarant is still under stress of excitement or shock

            3. The stress or shock must be sufficient to still the declarant’s ability to reflect or prevaricate. Kutza v. Bovis Lend Lease, 95 A.D. 3d 590; People v Swinger, 689 N.Y.S. 2d 336.

        2. Present Sense Impression

      See Lee v. New York Hospital Queens, 118 A.D. 3d 750.

       

    2. Testimony at Prior Trial — CPLR 451

      1. Trial of the same subject matter in same or another action between the same parties or their representatives.

      2. Witness must be unavailable as set out in statute

       

    3. Demonstrative Evidence

        1. Photographs,  Video, and Audio

          1. Photos, audio or video recordings must be authenticated by a person familiar with the object/scene.

          2. The witness must testify that the exhibit is a true, correct and/or accurate representation of the object/scene portrayed. However, with videos, in some cases testimony about the making of the video or chain of custody may be required.

          3. Audio recordings must not be altered or edited and the witness must be able to identify the speaker’s voice. Transcripts may be admitted, but if the recording is available it is the best evidence.

          4. Grucci v. Grucci, 20 N.Y. 3d 893; Das v. Sun Wah Restaurant, 94 A.D. 3d 752; People v. France, 120 A.D. 3d 1357; Morreale v. Froelich, 125 A.D. 3d 1280.

        1. Diagrams, Maps, and Models

          1. Must be a true representation of what it purports to represent, if not to scale, it must be accurate and correct for it proposed use (i.e., to illustrate the witness’ testimony, to aid the fact-finder or as direct proof of a fact)

          2. Maps may be admissible as public records (see paragraph F, below).

        2. Experiments and Test Results

      The technique or methodology of the test must be:

          1. Reliable

          2. Generally accepted by the scientific community; and 3) performed under conditions similar to those at the time of the occurrence.

        1. DNA or Genetic Marker Tests — CPLR 4518(d), (e)

          1. The test results, if certified, are prima facie evidence of the facts contained therein.

          2. If the certified test results show a probability of paternity of 95% or greater, the report creates a rebuttable presumption of paternity. If no objections are filed, the report established the paternity of the child.

       

    4. Private Writings

      1. Written agreements between the parties (contracts, leases, options, etc.) are admissible if properly authenticated.

      2. Private Memoranda, letters, journals, etc., if authenticated, are admissible if they meet the requirements as an admission or declaration (see paragraph A, above).

      3. Past recollection recorded – a writing of a witness may be admissible if:

          1.  It was made at or about the time of the act, event or occurrence recorded

          1. The witness can attest NOW that it was accurate at the time it was made

          2. The witness is presently unable to recall the facts.

       

    5. Public Documents, Records, and Reports

      1. Public records include deeds, state agency records, UCC filings, etc.

      2. Public records may be admitted under the Business Records exception, the statutory Public Records exception (CPLR 2307) or the common law exception.

      3. The CPLR requires that public records be “authenticated” (certified) as a true and complete copy of the records or reports contained. A certified public record is “self-authenticating,” that is, it needs no foundation testimony for admittance.

      4. The common law rule applies where a public officer is under a duty by statute or by the nature of his or her office to maintain a record or create a report of the acts or transactions occurring in the course of his or her official duty. The records or reports made under the supervision of his or her office are admissible into evidence. People v. Smith, 258 A.D. 2d 245; People v. Baker, 705 N.Y. 2d 846.

      5. The exception applies to facts, events or data, not opinions.

      6. Foreign public records may be authenticated pursuant to CPLR 4542. Matter of Eggers, 471 NYS2d 570.

       

    6. Computer Records

      1. Under the common law, computer records generally qualified as business records. The person entering the data did not need to be produced to give foundational testimony. People v. Markowitz, 721 N.Y. 2d 758.

      1. In 2002, CPLR 4518(a) was amended to provide rules for the admissibility of computer records. Printouts of computer records are subject to the same 4-part test as any other business record, plus an additional requirement that the printout be certified as a true and accurate representation of the electronic record.

      1. In addition, the court may consider the method or manner by which the electronic record was stored, maintained or retrieved in determining whether the exhibit is a true and accurate representation of such electronic record.

       

    7. Cell Phone Messages and Texts

      1. Cell phone instant messages may be authenticated by a participant in the conversation testifying that the other party’s name came up on his/her phone, and that the “cut and paste” document or photos of the messages offered to the court fairly and accurately represented the exchange between the parties. People v. Agudelo, 96 A.D. 3d 611.

      2. Text messages may be self-authenticating if the content reliably indicates that the other party was the sender. Photos of the texts are admissible if the witness testifies that he/she saw the photos taken and that they accurately and fairly reproduce the texts from the phone screen. People v. Green, 107 A.D. 3d 915.

       

    8. Business Records

        1. General Rule: General Rule: CPLR 4518 (a)

            1. Foundation:

                1. The record was made and kept in the regular course of business

                2. It was the “regular course of business” (an established business practice or procedure) to make the record

                3. The record was made at or near the time of the act or event recorded

                4. The person making the record had a business duty to report or record the data

                5. People v. Ramos, 13 N.Y. 3d 914; People v. Cordova, 127 A.D. 3d 757; Tougher Industries Ins. v. Dormitory Authority, 130 A.D. 3d 1397 (e-mail as a business record).

            2. All other circumstances of the making of the memorandum or record, including lack of personal knowledge by the maker, may be proved to affect its weight, but they shall not affect its admissibility.

            3. However, even if a business record meeting all the requirements of CPLR 4518 (a), it may still be inadmissible if it violates another rule of evidence.

            4. Double Hearsay – If the record contains voluntary hearsay statements made by 3rd persons not engaged in business or under any duty in relation to the business, the exception generally does not apply. People v. Boatwright, 748 N.Y. 2d 541; People v. Reynoso, 73 N.Y. 2d 816

            5. Double hearsay may be admissible in certain circumstances:

              1. If the 3rd party statement is admissible under another rule, the double hearsay is admissible.

              2. Medical records:

                      • Records, reports, including opinions, of other physicians, test results and laboratory reports are admissible if related to the diagnosis or treatment of the patient. Rodriguez v. Piccone, 5 A.D. 3d 757, Moran v. Demarinis, 152 A.D. 2d 546.

                      • Patient statements, including statements about the cause of the accident or injury, are only admissible to the extent they are related to diagnosis, prognosis or treatment. People v. Ortega, 15 N.Y. 3d 610; Matter of Dolan, 35 Misc. 3d 781.

                  1.  If the 3rd party statement is sufficiently “trustworthy,” it may be admissible as part of the record. People v. Settles, 46 N.Y. 2d 154

                1. The substantial familiarity rule:

                  1. The out-of-court statement must have indicia of reliability characteristic of a business record – the elements set out in CPLR 4518.

                  2. The witness must be substantially familiar with the business practices of the entity making the record. The witness does not need to know the specific record keeping practices if he/she is familiar with the statutory or regulatory requirements governing the making of the record or if it is made to suit the witness’ employer’s requirements.

                  3. The witness or the entity holding the record or document must routinely rely on the records of the entity making the records.

                  4. People v. Cratsley, 86 N.Y. 2d 81; People v. DiSalvo, 727 N.Y.S. 2d 146; Portfolio Recovery Assoc. v. Lall, 127 A.D. 3d 576; Deutsche Bank v. Monica, 131 A.D. 3d 737.

           

        1. Hospital Bills – CPLR 4518 (b)

          A hospital bill is admissible in evidence under this rule and is prima facie evidence of the facts contained, provided:

              1. It bears a certification by the head of the hospital or by a responsible employee in the controller’s or accounting office that the bill is correct

              2. Each of the items was necessarily supplied

              3. The amount charged is reasonable

        1. Other Records – CPLR 4518 (c)

          1. All records, writings and other things referred to in sections 2306 and 2307 are admissible in evidence under this rule and are prima facie evidence of the facts contained, provided they bear a certification or authentication by the head of the hospital, laboratory, department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or by an employee delegated for that purpose or by a qualified physician.

          2. CPLR 2306 -records relating to the condition or treatment of a patient held by a hospital, a department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or an officer thereof, requiring the production

          3. CPLR 2307 – records of a library, or a department or bureau of a municipal corporation or of the state, or an officer thereof

           

        2. SCU payment and disbursement records – CPLR 4518 (f)

          1. The records are admissible if:

            1. they bear a certification by an official of a social services district attesting to the accuracy of the content of the record or report of support payments;

            2. in attesting to the accuracy of the record or report such official has received confirmation from the department of social services or the fiscal agent that the record or report of support payments reflects the processing of all support payments in the possession of the department or the fiscal agent as of a specified date; and

            3.  the document is a record or report of support payments maintained pursuant to title six-A of article three of the social services law.

          2. If so certified, such record or report shall be admitted into evidence under this rule without the need for additional foundation testimony.

          3. Confirmation of payments requests are made to the processing center, by CMU inquiry via ECS, to confirm that payments for a specific NCP have been processed, and there are no “unprocessed” payments as of a certain date holding with the payment Processing Center or in the fiscal agent account.

          4. Such records shall be the basis for a permissive inference of the facts contained therein unless the trier of fact finds good cause not to draw such inference.

           

        3. Confinement costs – CPLR 4518 (g)

          Confinement costs may be proven in 3 ways:

              1. Medical bills under 4518 (b)

              2. If the bills were paid by Medicaid, as a public record under CPLR 4518 (c) and 2307

              3. Under CPLR 4518 (g) -Any hospital bills or records relating to the costs of pregnancy or birth of a child are admissible in evidence and are prima facie evidence of the facts contained therein, provided they bear a certification or authentication by the head of the hospital, laboratory, department or bureau of a municipal corporation or the state or by an employee designated for that purpose, or by a qualified physician.
