Calculating a Child Support Obligation Amount

You can find the latest poverty level and self-support reserve figures at:

https://www.childsupport.ny.gov/dcse/pdfs/CSSA.pdf

  1. Overview: Factors of the CSSA calculations

    There are four major factors the court will address when applying the CSSA guidelines to arrive at a child support obligation amount:

      • Gross income

      • Imputed income

      • Deductions

      • Needs of the children

  1. Adjusted annual income

    After reviewing the parents' income, the court will determine each parent's adjusted annual income. This is calculated by subtracting the CSSA allowable deductions from the total income (gross income plus imputed income).

  1. Deductions used in calculations of support order amount

    In applying the CSSA, the court uses the total income (gross income plus imputed income) previously established, and deducts the following:

      • New York City or Yonkers income taxes actually paid

      • FICA (Social Security and Medicare) taxes actually paid

      • Court-ordered alimony paid by the NCP

      • Child support payments made by the NCP to someone who is not a party to the court action

      • Certain unreimbursed business expenses

  1. Imputed income used in calculations of support order obligation amount

    While court uses the financial documentation that each parent provides, it can also use imputed income when determining a support obligation. Imputed income is non-monetary income that the court assigns a monetary value. These

    Examples of imputed income might include:

      • Workers compensation benefits

      • Fringe benefits

      • Monies provided by relatives and friends

      • Disability payments

      • Unemployment insurance benefits

      • Veterans benefits

      • Pension and retirement benefits

      • Fellowships and stipends

      • Annuity payments

      • Non-recurring payments as income, such as gifts and inheritances, lottery winnings, and life insurance policies.

      • A company car provided to an employee

      • Rental properties owned

      • Shares of stock owned

      • Private tutoring, or supplemental jobs

    In additional to gross income and imputed income, the court may consider willful reduction of income, and calculate accordingly.

  1. CSSA assigned percentages

    The amount of the adjusted annual income is multiplied by a percentage (below) based upon the number of children on the case.

    Percentage of Income

    Number of Children on Case

    17%

    One child

    25%

    Two children

    29%

    Three children

    31%

    Four children

    35%

    Five or more children

  1. Poverty level guidelines

    If the basic child support obligation reduces the NCP's income below the annual poverty level amount of $12,140, the child support obligation amount will be set at $25 per month.

    If not, the court will determine if the obligation will reduce the NCP's income below the self-support reserve amount.

  1. Self-support reserve guidelines

    According to FCA 413, in the event that the basic obligation amount would reduce the NCP's income to fall below the self-support reserve but not below poverty level, the court will set the basic obligation amount at $50 per month or the difference between the NCP's income and the self-support reserve, whichever is greater.

  1. Define "self support reserve"

    The Family Court Act defines self-support reserve as follows:

    "'Self-support reserve' shall mean one hundred thirty-five percent (135%) of the poverty income guidelines amount for a single person as reported by the federal Department of Health and Human Services. On March 1st of each year, the self-support reserve shall be revised to reflect the annual updating of the poverty income guidelines as reported by the federal Department of Health and Human Services for a single person household."

  1. Deviation from the CSSA guidelines

    Although the court is required to apply the CSSA guidelines, it may use its discretion to deviate from the standards under extenuating circumstances. Some possible reasons are:

      • Financial resources of parents and child

      • The child's physical and emotional health

      • The standard of living if relationship had not ended

      • Tax consequences

      • Non-monetary contributions toward the child's well-being

      • Educational needs of either parent

      • Substantial differences in gross incomes

      • The needs of other children of the NCP

      • Extraordinary visitation expenses
